The Price of Progress:

Investigating the Nexus Between GDP Growth and Carbon Emissions in the Philippines

Our group, C0DEBABE5, intends to investigate the relationship between a country’s economic development and carbon emissions, using existing data mapping our and other regions’ economic growth to provide a quantitative analysis on where the Philippines might be standing.

Overview

Introduction

While economic growth is often celebrated for bringing prosperity and development, the Philippines' rapid ascent as a developing powerhouse raises important questions about sustainability. The country's impressive strides in GDP are undeniably positive, but if unchecked, this growth may come at a significant environmental cost. This is why we decided to focus on this issue: to investigate whether the Philippines' economic progress is contributing to rising carbon emissions and environmental degradation. Our project aims to uncover the delicate balance between economic success and environmental stewardship, emphasizing the need for sustainable growth to ensure a healthier future.

Overview

Problem and Response

A rapidly growing economy with concerns about its environmental impacts

With this, our group C0DEBABE5 has conducted an investigation on the Philippines' CO2 emissions over time and a comparative analysis on its performance relative to other countries.

Overview

Research Questions

How do the Philippines' CO2 emissions from cement and fossil fuels correlate with its GDP over time, and are these emission trends aligned with the rates of economic growth from 1946 to 2022?

Does the Philippines emit CO2 disproportionately compared to its GDP and GDP growth, relative to other and similar economies?

Overview

Intention

C0DEBABE5 seeks to relate the Philippine's fossil fuel and cement CO2 emissions, separately, to the Philippines GDP growth, and compare the Philippines' GDP emissions, in terms of cement CO2 emissions and fossil fuel CO2 emissions, to those of other developing countries and some developed country. The group aims to collect and combine data from various creible sources, but primarily use data from Our World in Data. CO2 emissions from OWID are in million tonnes, and the GDP values are in international dollars benchmarked at 2011, rather than current US Dollars, in order to prevent inflation from distorting our analysis. Our analysis is based on a statistical regression test for the Philippines, and a t-test on a sampling distribution to compare the Philippines' emissions vs GDP coefficients to those of other countries.

The Data

Sources

The World Bank Dataset

The World Bank Data website is a platform provided by the World Bank Group that offers free access to a wide range of data on global development. The team had acquired several necessary development indicators from this data source, particularly economy-related data for correlation purposes.

OWID CO2 Emissions Dataset

This dataset contains information on global carbon dioxide (CO2) emissions over time across various countries. Such data consists of metrics obtained from Our World in Data and its organization. Such data consists of indicators concerning Greenhouse gas emissions to be correlated to other data features.

Size

The combined dataset created from the two data sources resulted into a size of 1111 data points. Note that this size was the result after data filtering and pre-processing

Time Period

The data obtained are filtered between the years 1946-2022 in adherence to data recency and availability.

Countries

The countries included in this project consist of: Philippines, Thailand, Vietnam, Malaysia, Indonesia, Cambodia, Mexico, Brazil, India, United States, Kenya, South Africa, Sri Lanka, Myanmar, China.

The Data

Features

The Data

Measures

Fossil Fuel CO2 Emissions

This feature consists of the sum of CO2 emissions from gas, coal, and oil sources. Energy production, industrial processes, and transportation are some examples of sectors of which fossil fuel combustion occurs of which are necessary for the economy to run and grow. Such data obtained from the OWID dataset are measured in million tonnes.

Gross Domestic Product

The Gross Domestic Product or GDP is an indicator used to measure the general economic performance of a country, making this feature to be highly relevant in the aims of the project. Following the dataset in OWID and The World Bank, the GDP is measured using the US Dollar currency.

Cement CO2 Emissions

This feature consists of CO2 emissions from the production of cement. Apart from the previous sources of CO2 emissions, cement production is a great contributing factor to generation of Greenhouse gasses, whereas about 8% of the total CO2 emissions are due to cement productions as reported by the CBS News last January 2023. It is also measured in million tonnes.

The Hypotheses

C0DEBABE5 has identified four particular hypotheses from the two research questions:

There is no (there exists a) significant relationship between GDP and fossil fuel CO2 emissions.

There is no (there exists a) significant relationship between GDP and cement CO2 emissions.

The Philippines' Fossil Fuel CO2 to GDP regression coefficient is not (is) significantly different from the mean CO2 to GDP regression coefficient of all developing countries in our dataset.

The Philippines' Cement CO2 to GDP regression coefficient is not (is) significantly different from the mean CO2 to GDP regression coefficient of all developing countries in our dataset.

Exploratory Data Analysis

Fossil Fuel & Cement CO2 emissions and GDP in the Philippines over time
Permutation-Regression Test for Philippine CO2 emissions and GDP

To further examine the relationship between GDP and CO2 emissions, a Permutation-Regression test was done between the two features. The result for both features yielded a positive relationship between the two (ccement ≈ 0.011; cfossil fuel ≈ 0.133). Such computed coefficients were also found to be statistically significant at a 95% confidence interval, hence leading to the rejection of the first two null hypotheses (p_valuecement ≈ 0.001; p_valuefossil fuel ≈ 0.001).

Philippines vs. Myanmar & Vietnam in Fossil Fuel & Cement CO2 emissions

These graphs compare the Philippines' GDP, cement CO2 emissions, and fossil fuel CO2 emissions to the most similar country in the dataset, Vietnam, and the most different country in the dataset, Myanmar.

Statistical Tests for Regression Coefficients

At a confidence level of 95%, the Philippines' regression coefficient for GDP to cement CO2 is not significantly different from the developing countries' mean with p = 0.08, but its regression coefficient for GDP to fossil fuel CO2 is significantly different from the mean of the developing countries' sampling distribution with p = 2x10(-180).

Results and Interpretation

Discussions from the results obtained in the methodology

Results from general data exploration

Interpreting the GDP-CO2 emission data using the International Dollar (Purchasing Power Parity - PPP) rather than USD provides a clearer and more meaningful comparison of the economic performance and environmental impact of the countries being compared. The relative price adjustment accounts for the difference in cost of living and price levels in different countries, providing a more accurate measure of purchasing power and actual GDP value in terms of economic welfare.

The Philippines has demonstrated consistent and rapid GDP growth, especially after the year 2000, suggesting significant improvements in the purchasing power of its population. Despite the significant economic expansion, the increase in CO2 emissions has been moderate.

Vietnam’s trajectory, however, tells a different story. While the country’s GDP has also risen significantly, their rise in CO2 emissions has also grown disproportionately, compared to the Philippines, suggesting a heavier reliance on fossil fuels driven by rapid expansion.

Myanmar, another country in Southeast Asia, with its much smaller economy and lower emissions presents an interesting case. The country’s GDP growth is a lot less pronounced and emissions have only barely started to noticeably rise in recent decades. The adjusted GDP shows Myanmar’s potential for sustainable growth, with opportunities to adopt sustainable practices from the get-go.

Overall, using PPP data for interpretation shows that the Philippines has achieved efficient economic growth with moderate CO2 emissions, suggesting that, at least in the aspect of greenhouse gas emissions, we’re doing relatively well.

Results from statistical analyses

Concerning the statistical tests' results. We outperform our most similar neighbor, Vietnam, both having a higher GDP and having lower CO2 emissions from both cement and fossil fuels. Our most different neighbor, Myanmar, has both a lower GDP and lower emissions, which precludes direct comparison.

Our permutation regression test reveals that there are indeed statistically significant relationships between the Philippines' GDP and its cement CO2 emissions, and between its GDP and its fossil fuel CO2 emissions.

The Philippines' regression coefficient for fossil fuel CO2 and GDP is significantly different from those of other developing countries, but the Philippines' regression coefficient for cement CO2 is not significantly different from those of other developing countries. Additionally, direct inspection of the regression coefficients reveals that the Philippines' coefficient for fossil fuel CO2 emission(y) vs GDP(x) is in fact the third lowest, ,having an even lower coefficient than the developed United States, with only Sri lanka and Myanmar having lower coefficients.

In our analysis, a lower coefficient is better because it translates to less of an emissions increase for a given GDP increase.

Conclusion

The CO2 emissions over GDP for the Philippines show a general decrease, indicating significant improvements in carbon efficiency. Compared to neighbors like Vietnam, the Philippines demonstrates better carbon efficiency. This trend aligns with global advancements driven by technological improvements and a shift towards sustainability. However, broader environmental and social challenges remain. To ensure truly sustainable and inclusive development, comprehensive policies addressing total emissions, ecological health, and social well-being are essential​.

Final Words

Despite the fact that the nation's carbon emissions over its GDP seem to be slower relative to other countries, the fact that GDP and CO2 emissions proportionally increase over time remains the same. Hence we, C0DEBABE5, maintain our continuous support for the advocacy of reducing carbon emissions in any form whilst simultaneously aiming for economic progress. This project intends to spread social awareness for the matter and encourage individuals and institutions to develop more enviroment-friendly procedures in all areas within the economy and its productions.
